Experimental Limit to the Electron Electric Dipole Moment Places Constraints on Models, Experiments, and Future Colliders


Summary

    Beyond Standard Model theories that predict Dark Matter, Baryon Asymmetry, Axion-like particles, and Supersymmetry, also predict electron electric dipole moments (EDMs). The experimental electron EDM upper limit can therefore constrain these theories. Below is a selection of publications examining the impact of the electron EDM upper limit on some of these models. Also examined is the impact of the electron EDM upper limit on muon (g-2) experiments and on a possible future muon collider.

  • Constraints on the muon EDM and the muon anomalous magnetic moment


    Interpretation of experimental results are also being subjected to constraints from the limits on the electron EDM. Take the case of the most recent series of measurements of the muon anomalous magnetic moment, which have been in disagreement with theory for nearly two decades.

    The Muon (g-2) Collaboration reported[1] that the Brookhaven E821 muon anomalous magnetic moment experiment had a disagreement with theory of 2.2 to 2.7 standard deviations. Among the very few explanations for the disagreement was a muon EDM which would cause a tilt in the spin precession plane of the muons. The EDM would not change (g-2) but would cause a systematic error in its measurement in E821. They found that ...a nonzero EDM of 2 x 10-19 e·cm would cause a systematic increase... in the measured anomalous magnetic moment by ...1.6 ppm. This would be sufficient to bring experiment E821 into agreement with theory.

    They noted however that the electron EDM upper bound, when scaled by the ratio of muon-to-electron mass, implies a muon EDM limit, orders of magnitude smaller than 10-19 e·cm. (Mechanisms that give rise to an electron EDM would also be expected to give rise to a larger muon EDM with the muon EDM being larger than the electron EDM by the ratio of the muon mass to the electron mass which is about 206, or to the square of the mass ratio which is 43,000: the power of the mass ratio needed, usually depending on the number of loops involved in the coupling.)

    Using the same apparatus but at different muon energy (which made their measurements more sensitive to an EDM), the Muon (g-2) Collaboration then set an upper bound[2] to the muon EDM of 1.8 x 10-19 e·cm [95% CL].

    The apparatus was subsequently moved to FNAL and the experiment continued using a more intense beam of positive muons. The smaller error bars from the improved and combined results[3] has increased the discrepancy with theory [4]. There are now suggestions that the muon and electron in Beyond Standard Models are decoupled[5].

  • Constraints on a Future Muon Collider


    The Snowmass cross-frontier Muon Collider Forum has issued a report [1] that includes the consideration of the relationship between an electron EDM and new (flavor and CP-violating) electroweak charged particles that couple to the Higgs. They give a scaling relationship between the particle mass and the resulting electron EDM (p 22, Eq. 2.9) where δcp is a CP-violating phase and M is the mass scale of the new particle)s). They note that ... a discovery of an EDM within the next two orders of magnitude in sensitivity would point to the few TeV scale for new physics. The particles responsible for the EDM could thus be pair-produced at a multi-TeV muon collider.

    But what if the limit to the electron EDM is lowered by two orders of magnitude, as has been proposed, and no electron EDM is found? Will that imply there is no new physics to be found at the few TeV scale of a new collider? What if there is a false positive result? Might that contribute to a suboptimal choice of collider energy? And a center of mass muon collider of 3 TeV [2] may be more affected than one with 10 TeV.
